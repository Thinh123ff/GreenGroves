    Gardening - The Art of Beautifying Your Home

    Gardens beautify your home and are a great way to relax after work. If you're new to gardening, build your garden with the following easy steps:

    1

    Choose the right location

    It's always better to start small for big success. Choose a small space to start building your garden. Make sure your chosen space gets 5-6 hours of direct sunlight. Avoid windy spots, as wind can topple young and growing plants. Wind can also prevent pollinators from doing their job. Finally, consider the accessibility of your garden for watering, harvesting, and care. As the saying goes, "out of sight, out of mind."

    2

    Choose your garden type

    After determining the right location for your garden, the next step in your gardening journey is to choose the type of garden you want. Do you want a sea of vibrant flowers, a beautiful herb area, a kitchen garden for the aspiring chef in you, or a nutritious vegetable garden to stay healthy and fit? Whatever you choose, start small to paint your perfect garden picture.

    3

    Improve your soil

    Plants always benefit from nutrient-rich garden soil. Start by checking your soil's texture; it should be easy to dig and crumble in your hand. If your soil is hard and clay-like, roots will struggle to grow. If your soil is rocky, till and remove the rocks. Improving soil quality isn't as hard as you think, and it brings many benefits. Add compost made from tea leaves, vegetable peels to the soil to enhance quality.

    4

    Prepare basic tools

    Once you have a plan, you'll need some basic gardening tools to get started. Here's a list of some essentials: Must-haves: A tool every gardener should own is pruning shears. You'll use them to trim plants and shrubs, and keep plants healthy by cutting off dead parts. Digging tools: You'll need a few tools to dig and prepare the soil before planting. These include a shovel, trowel, and garden rake. Shovels and trowels are for digging holes, while the rake is for breaking up large clumps of soil or clearing old roots and weeds. Watering tools: The best tools for watering your garden are hoses and watering cans. Hoses are great for big jobs like watering trees and large areas. For small and sensitive plants, a watering can is better. Young plants will thank you for gentle watering. Weeding tools: To handle weeds and remove unwanted plants, you'll need a sharp-tipped trowel and a garden knife. These two tools help keep your garden tidy.

    5

    Choose the right plants

    This is the most exciting part of gardening - choosing your greenery. Before rushing to plant anything, take time to research plants. Some plants love direct sunlight, while others prefer shade. You can check this information on seed packets. Choosing native plants for your area will make things easier, especially if you're about to become a "plant parent." You can also look at your neighbor's garden to see what grows well or read our guide on the best vegetables to plant by month. These ways will give you ideas about which plants will thrive in your garden.

    6

    Plan your layout

    Before you start planting, make a plan! Planning: Draw a map for each plant's location, paying attention to spacing. Like people, plants need their own space. If you plant seedlings too close together, their growth may be stunted, they may get sick, or even die. Labeling: We all forget sometimes. To know what you planted where and to identify them, make small labels and place them next to the plants. You can get creative with labels for a unique touch. Organizing: A great tip for beginners is to make your own garden notebook and track progress. You can add drawings, photos, labels, and notes to monitor each plant's development in different areas, then make progress reports to understand plant characteristics.

    7

    Design your beds/plots

    After planning, decide on the type and size of your garden beds. Raised beds look attractive and are easier to work with. Gardening in beds or blocks is more convenient than single rows. Beds should be 3-4 feet wide, narrow enough to reach the middle from both sides. The length should be about 8-10 feet, making it easy to move around without stepping on the planting area. Start small and leave space for each plant to grow. Seeds and seedlings are small, but mature plants can grow large, making the area crowded and hard to thrive. In beds, arrange plants in rows or grids. The goal is to reduce walkways and maximize planting space. This also saves time and money by only fertilizing and improving the soil in the planting area.

    8

    Plant properly

    Once you're ready, start planting. Most seed packets have basic instructions. Try it and you'll gradually master planting skills. Basic rule: Plant seeds at a depth 3-4 times their diameter, unless the packet says otherwise. Cover seeds with soil and water thoroughly, making sure seeds aren't exposed. Transplanting: When moving seedlings from pots to their final spot, dig a hole twice as wide as the root ball. Loosen the soil, add organic fertilizer to boost growth. Place the root ball and cover the roots completely with soil. Water lightly after transplanting.

    9

    Water properly

    The goal of watering is to provide enough water for plants to grow, but overwatering can cause root rot and harm plants. The best way is to water slowly, letting water soak deep into the soil. The soil should be moist at a depth of 3-4 inches. Plants need more water in hot summer. Read our guide on summer watering for optimal plant growth. Plants at different growth stages need different amounts of water. Seedlings need daily watering to encourage growth and strong roots, while mature plants need watering every 2-3 days, depending on weather conditions.

    10

    Add organic fertilizer

    There's an old saying: "Fertilizer is a gardener's best friend." Make your own organic fertilizer to boost plant growth. Start with compost - organic material you can add to your garden to help plants grow. You can use tea bags, ground coffee, grass clippings, crushed fruit peels, etc. Thêm phân hữu cơ vào đất giúp giữ ẩm, kích thích vi khuẩn tốt, chống sâu bệnh và giảm dấu chân carbon của bạn.

    11

    Natural pest control

    Pests are often attracted to stressed or undernourished plants. If your plants are healthy and well-nourished, pest problems will be less. If plants get infected, there are organic solutions. You can make your own pesticide at home by mixing neem oil, water, and a few drops of dish soap. Shake well and spray on plants every 2 weeks to repel pests!

    12

    Mulch with organic material

    Depending on what you plant, consider the best mulch. Why? Mulch provides nutrients to the soil and protects against erosion. Mulch is material spread or laid on the soil surface. It helps retain moisture, keeps the soil cool, prevents weeds, and makes garden beds look better. As organic mulch breaks down, it also improves soil fertility.

    13

    Start with easy-to-grow vegetables

    Nothing beats fresh vegetables from your own garden. Juicy, delicious veggies you can pick and eat right away. Each region has different planting times based on the weather, and each vegetable has its own temperature preferences. Start with easy-to-grow veggies like tomatoes, radishes, bell peppers, herbs, and leafy greens like lettuce, kale, rocket, chili. Once you're skilled, you can try growing cabbage, broccoli which require more time and effort. Choose beginner-friendly vegetable seeds and order to grow your own kitchen garden.

    14

    Container gardening

    Container gardening is a great way to grow plants when space is limited. Almost any flower, vegetable, herb, shrub can be grown in containers. Dwarf and compact plants are suitable for small pots. Choose plants that match the climate and the amount of sun or shade the container receives. Basil, chives, thyme, and other herbs are great for pots, placed right outside the kitchen for convenience. Whatever pot you choose, drainage holes are a must. Without good drainage, soil can get waterlogged and plants will die. The holes don't need to be large, but enough for excess water to escape. You'll need to water pots more often than in-ground plants, as potting soil dries out faster.

    15

    Window box gardening

    Don't worry if you have a small garden or no garden at all - you can still grow greenery. Window boxes can grow everything from fruits, vegetables to perennials and annuals. They brighten up your home's exterior with flowers and greenery. Of course, not just for small spaces - large gardens also benefit from the color and height variation they bring.

    16

    Companion planting

    Like people, plants have their "companions." Companion planting is growing different plants together in the same area to optimize space, repel pests, and provide nutrients. Some species thrive when grown together, while others may inhibit each other's growth. For example, tomatoes yield more (and repel mosquitoes, flies) when grown with basil. Tomato "allies" include asparagus, carrots, celery, onions, lettuce, marigolds, parsley, and spinach. Avoid planting tomatoes near cabbage, beets, corn, dill, potatoes, and rosemary.

    17

    Prune and shape properly

    Proper maintenance is wonderful and brings the greatest rewards for your garden. Take time to weed, prune, and tidy up the garden. If you see slow-growing plants, check the roots by gently digging around. Remove pests with appropriate measures. Water regularly and add nutrients periodically. That's it, starting a garden isn't as scary as you think. Whether you're a gardening expert or not, you can enjoy a blooming garden every year with good planning, careful plant selection, and soil nutrition. Order seeds online and get started!

    18

    Maintain and care regularly

    Regularly weed, prune leaves, check for pests, water, and fertilize to keep your garden lush and green.

    Quick Tips
    • Start with a small space
    • Choose plants suitable for the climate
    • Water properly
    • Use organic fertilizer
    • Check for pests regularly
